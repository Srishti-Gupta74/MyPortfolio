They fundamentally fail to answer the more important one: 'How much should I trust that forecast?' This gap — between what a model predicts and how reliable that prediction actually is — conceals critical information from every downstream inventory decision in a supply chain. The concealment is dangerous because inventory decisions are asymmetric under uncertainty. A forecast of 1,200 units made with high confidence warrants a different safety-stock response than the same number produced under deep uncertainty. Standard point forecasts make these situations look identical. The operator has no mechanism to interrogate whether the model understands the current demand regime. In stable categories, this miscalibration is costly but recoverable. In essential-goods categories — food, medicine, emergency materials — the same flaw causes severe community harm. The causal chain is direct: a COVID-like demand shock occurs, the static forecasting model extrapolates outside its training distribution, its uncertainty estimates collapse silently without signalling degradation, the operator sets safety stock based on apparently confident intervals, and essential goods run short at peak community need. This happened to PPE, sanitisers, and food staples during COVID-19 — not because demand was unknowable, but because inventory systems had no mechanism to say 'I am less certain than usual.' The specific failure runs deeper than inaccurate point forecasts. Even when organisations use probabilistic forecasting tools, uncertainty estimates are calibrated on stable historical data. When a demand shock breaks the distributional assumptions underlying that calibration, the resulting intervals become too narrow — a stated 90% interval may achieve only 65% empirical coverage during a shock — and the operator receives no signal this has happened. The forecasting system knows less than it appears to, and it has no way to say so. I am asking: can a supply chain forecasting system produce uncertainty estimates that remain statistically honest even during the non-stationary demand conditions that define real-world crises — and translate that honesty into an inventory decision a manager can act on before the stockout, not after? Proposed Solution: I propose SupplyShield AI: an adaptive decision-support system that converts honest uncertainty into immediate inventory action. Where traditional systems predict demand and assume confidence, SupplyShield AI follows a four-step process: predict demand, quantify uncertainty, verify whether that uncertainty is calibrated under current conditions, and convert the result into a specific, defensible inventory decision. The technical core is Adaptive Conformal Inference (ACI), proposed by Gibbs and Candès (2022). Standard conformal prediction provides formal coverage guarantees only when the calibration set is exchangeable with the test distribution — an assumption that breaks during demand shocks. ACI replaces this with an adaptive feedback mechanism: it maintains a miscoverage level α_t that updates after each observation. When the true demand falls outside the predicted interval, α_t decreases and future intervals widen automatically; when it falls inside, α_t increases slightly. This provides long-run coverage guarantees without distributional assumptions — meaning SupplyShield AI stays honest even during the demand shocks that cause static methods to silently fail. I implement and compare three UQ methods: LightGBM quantile regression as a non-conformal baseline, static conformal prediction as the principled-but-stationary benchmark, and ACI as the primary engine. All three are evaluated identically on the M5 Competition and DataCo Smart Supply Chain datasets, with results reported separately for stable and volatile demand periods. Rather than outputting a raw statistical interval, SupplyShield AI delivers a structured decision package per SKU: a Forecast Reliability Score (0–100) expressing current calibration quality in a number any manager can interpret; a risk tier (Low, Medium, High); and a specific safety-stock recommendation derived from the ACI interval through the newsvendor formula — connecting uncertainty directly to inventory action through a principled quantitative mechanism, not an arbitrary multiplier. The ACI update rule is now mathematically defined: α_{t+1} = α_t + γ(α − 1{y_t ∉ Ĉ_t}), where α is the target miscoverage rate (e.g., 0.10 for 90% coverage), γ is a step-size parameter selected by grid search over {0.001, 0.005, 0.01, 0.05} on the validation set, and 1{y_t ∉ Ĉ_t} equals 1 when the true demand fell outside the interval at time t. When coverage degrades during a shock, α_t falls automatically, widening future intervals before the stockout, not after. Gibbs and Candès (2022) prove long-run marginal coverage at rate α for any observation sequence without distributional assumptions. The newsvendor safety-stock derivation: given ACI 90% interval [L_t, U_t], implied demand standard deviation σ̂_t = (U_t − L_t) / (2 × 1.645). Safety stock: SS_t = z_SL × σ̂_t × √L, where z_SL is the service-level z-score and L is lead time in periods. This replaces arbitrary multipliers with a formula grounded in standard inventory theory. The Forecast Reliability Score is a weighted composite on a 0–100 scale: 50% weight on rolling empirical coverage rate, 30% weight on calibration error (mean absolute difference between stated and observed coverage), and a 20% volatility-regime penalty when demand CV exceeds 0.30. A score of 92/100 signals near-optimal performance; a score of 58/100 during a detected demand shock tells the manager in plain terms to apply conservative safety-stock logic — because SupplyShield AI itself is signalling reduced confidence in its current environment. Both datasets are named: M5 Competition (30,490 SKUs, 5.4 years) as primary and DataCo Smart Supply Chain (18,000+ records) as secondary, testing whether ACI's advantage generalises across structurally different supply chain contexts. Prototype Idea Problem Formulation: Supply chain forecasting systems suffer from a systemic design flaw: they produce point forecasts without providing operators a verifiable measure of confidence. During stable periods, this miscalibration causes manageable excess inventory. During non-stationary demand shocks — COVID-19 supply runs, extreme weather events, sudden supply disruptions — this flaw becomes catastrophic. Models trained on historical data remain confidently wrong; intervals become dangerously narrow; and supply chain managers, blind to degraded reliability, fail to order sufficient safety stock. Essential goods — medical supplies, food staples, emergency materials — stock out exactly when vulnerable communities need them most. The core problem has two layers that must be solved together. The algorithmic layer: static uncertainty quantification methods, including standard conformal prediction, provide coverage guarantees contingent on distributional stationarity. This guarantee breaks during demand shocks — a stated 90% interval may achieve only 60–65% actual coverage — without any signal reaching the operator. The operational layer: even when uncertainty is accurately quantified, a translation failure prevents managers from acting on it under time pressure. A manager during a crisis cannot interpret calibration curves. She needs one number telling her how much to trust the forecast, and one recommendation telling her how many units to order. SupplyShield AI is built to solve both layers: an adaptive algorithmic layer that maintains honest uncertainty estimates under non-stationarity, and an operational layer that translates those estimates into an immediate, unambiguous inventory directive — a Forecast Reliability Score, a risk tier, and a specific safety-stock recommendation — delivered through a Risk Command Center dashboard that requires zero statistical expertise to act on. Proposed Solution: SupplyShield AI is a five-component adaptive pipeline delivering a complete inventory action per SKU. 1. Data Pipeline: Processes M5 Competition (primary — 30,490 SKUs, daily, 5.4 years, Kaggle) and DataCo Smart Supply Chain (secondary — 18,000+ multi-channel order records, Kaggle) into feature-engineered demand series — lag features at 7, 14, 28 days; rolling mean and standard deviation; day-of-week, month, year; promotional flags. Volatility regime labels (stable: CV ≤ 0.30; volatile: CV > 0.30) applied per product-window using rolling-origin splits with no data leakage. 2. Base Forecasting Layer: LightGBM trained on MAE loss via rolling-origin cross-validation — serves as the point-forecast baseline and base estimator for both conformal methods. 3. Adaptive UQ Layer: Three methods in parallel — LightGBM quantile regression (alpha = 0.05/0.95), static MAPIE conformal prediction, and ACI via the Gibbs-Candès update rule implemented in approximately 50 lines of Python with no specialised library dependency. All produce identical output formats so the comparison isolates the uncertainty method, not the presentation. 4. Decision Layer: Derives σ̂_t = (U_t − L_t)/(2 × 1.645) from the ACI interval; computes SS_t = z_SL × σ̂_t × √L; calculates the Forecast Reliability Score (50% rolling coverage rate + 30% inverse calibration error + 20% volatility-regime adjustment, normalised 0–100); maps to Low/Medium/High risk tier; assigns escalation level: Standard Reorder, Manual Review, or Escalate to Procurement. 5. Risk Command Center (Streamlit): A single operations screen per SKU showing — point forecast, ACI prediction interval, Forecast Reliability Score with colour-coded status (green ≥ 80 / amber 60–79 / red < 60), risk tier, newsvendor safety-stock recommendation, escalation directive, calibration health (at-target / degrading / recovering), and rolling α_t trajectory. One screen. Zero statistical expertise required. Technical feasibility: LightGBM trains on M5 in under five minutes on CPU. MAPIE reduces static conformal to under 20 lines of code. ACI is 50 lines of Python from first principles — no specialised library needed. Both datasets are freely available on Kaggle. The Forecast Reliability Score and dashboard are post-processing on top of the UQ layer, adding hours to the build, not days. Stack: Python, pandas, LightGBM, MAPIE, scikit-learn, matplotlib, Streamlit — fully reproducible via YAML config and fixed random seeds. Expected impact: SupplyShield AI delivers a measurable reduction in calibration error during high-volatility periods, translated into concrete inventory outcomes — reduced stockout rates and optimised safety stock — quantified by comparing ACI-informed decisions against a point-forecast baseline on held-out test segments of both datasets. The public-good consequence is direct: a supply chain manager whose Reliability Score drops during a demand shock receives an automatic signal to buffer more, before the stockout, not after. For food, medicine, and emergency materials, this timing difference determines whether communities have access to essentials during crises. Because ACI wraps any existing forecaster without retraining, SupplyShield AI is domain-agnostic and immediately deployable on top of any organisation's current stack — making the path from prototype to real-world impact low-cost and achievable. — End of Submission — SupplyShield AI
